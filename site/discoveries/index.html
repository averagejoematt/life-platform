// how to read this
r value: Pearson correlation coefficient. r=1.0 = perfect positive relationship; r=0 = no relationship; r=−1.0 = perfect inverse. In practice, r≥0.4 is a moderate relationship, r≥0.6 is strong — especially for noisy biometric data.  FDR correction: When testing 23+ pairs simultaneously, you'd expect ~1 false positive at p<0.05 by chance alone. The Benjamini-Hochberg correction raises the significance bar proportionally, so only pairs that clear the adjusted threshold are flagged. A finding marked "FDR" survived that stricter test.  Why N=1 still matters: Population studies average out individual variation. These correlations are computed on a single person's data — meaning between-person variance is eliminated entirely. The signal-to-noise ratio is often better than a small clinical trial.

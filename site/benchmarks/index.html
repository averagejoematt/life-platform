// the framework

Why These Numbers?

Dr. Peter Attia's research on all-cause mortality identifies muscle mass and cardiovascular fitness as two of the strongest predictors of longevity — more predictive than most biomarkers, including many cholesterol panels. The Centenarian Decathlon flips the conventional question: instead of asking "what should I train for now," it asks "what do I need to be able to do at 85?"

The framework accounts for the inevitable: muscle mass declines 3–5% per decade after age 35, and VO2 max falls roughly 1% per year. To function at an elite 75-year-old level at age 85, you need to build enough reserve now that the decline still leaves you capable. These targets are calculated backward from that end-state — they are not arbitrary performance goals.

// the math

Why Relative Strength Matters

Absolute numbers are irrelevant across different body sizes. A 400 lb deadlift means something very different for a 150 lb person vs a 300 lb person. Bodyweight-relative targets normalize for size.

// the multipliers explained
Lift Multiplier Rationale
Deadlift 2.0× BW Posterior chain, hip hinge — picking things up
Squat 1.75× BW Leg drive — standing from chairs, stairs
Bench 1.25× BW Pressing — pushing off the floor, doors
OHP 0.75× BW Overhead reach — shelving, overhead bins
Carry 1.0× BW Grip + gait — luggage, groceries
// the backward calculation

Attia's model: decide what you want to be able to do at 85, then work backward through expected decline rates to determine what you need to achieve now.

Example at current trajectory:
If you're 38 now and want a 1.0× BW deadlift at 85 (functional pickup), and expect 3% muscle loss per decade over 47 years (~14% total loss), you need roughly 1.16× BW deadlift today as your maintenance floor.

The 2.0× BW target builds reserve above that floor — accounting for non-linear decline, injury risk, and the goal of being not just functional but robust at 85.

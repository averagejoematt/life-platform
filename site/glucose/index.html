Glucose Observatory

Most people never know their glucose response to food. Here’s mine, over 30+ weeks of CGM data.

// what a CGM reveals

What fasting glucose doesn't tell you

A fasting glucose test gives you one number — a snapshot taken after 8+ hours without food. It captures none of the metabolic action: not what happens after meals, not whether glucose spikes at 2am, not whether a salad or a steak drives different post-meal curves in your body specifically.

A continuous glucose monitor (CGM) reads glucose every 5 minutes, 24 hours a day. That's 288 data points per day versus one. The additional signal includes glucose variability (low variability = stable metabolism), post-meal spike magnitude, nocturnal patterns, and how specific foods interact with your individual physiology — not population averages.

Time-in-range (TIR) — the percentage of readings between 70 and 180 mg/dL — is now considered a more actionable metric than HbA1c for lifestyle optimization because it responds to intervention within days, not months.

// Matthew's CGM context

Device: Dexcom Stelo — worn continuously, no prescription required. Replaced every 15 days. Data syncs automatically to life-platform.

03
Protein-first meals moved the needle most. Eating protein and fat before carbohydrates blunts the post-meal spike by slowing gastric emptying.
04

05
Reducing refined carbs (bread, white rice, sweetened drinks) eliminated the majority of >140 readings. Whole-food carbs with fiber produce much smaller spikes.

Why variability matters
Glucose variability
Standard deviation (SD) measures how much glucose swings day-to-day. Even with a normal average, high variability indicates metabolic stress — large spikes and crashes that don't show up in HbA1c. Target: SD < 20 mg/dL (longevity medicine consensus). Lower is better. Stable glucose = stable energy, mood, and cognition.
Data source
Dexcom Stelo CGM
Continuous glucose monitoring via Dexcom Stelo — a reading every 5 minutes, 288 data points per day. Synced to Apple Health, ingested nightly into life-platform DynamoDB. Daily aggregates computed: avg, min, max, SD, time-in-range (70–180 mg/dL), and longevity optimal range (70–120 mg/dL).

Measurement Protocol
SENSOR Dexcom Stelo OTC CGM. 15-day wear cycle. 5-minute reading interval. No calibration required.
TIME IN RANGE (TIR) Standard: 70–180 mg/dL (ADA guideline for non-diabetic). Optimal: 70–120 mg/dL (longevity medicine target, stricter). Target: >90% standard, >70% optimal.
VARIABILITY (SD) Standard deviation of all 5-min readings in a 24-hour period. SD <15: excellent. 15–20: good. >20: elevated metabolic stress. N=1 data — individual baselines vary.

Nocturnal Glucose Patterns

What happens to glucose while you sleep reveals metabolic health that daytime readings can’t capture. The dawn phenomenon, overnight stability, and how sleep architecture shapes nighttime glucose are all visible in CGM data.

Dawn Phenomenon
A natural glucose rise between 4–7 AM driven by cortisol and growth hormone. Normal in healthy individuals. Tracking magnitude over time to establish personal baseline. Currently seeing 10–18 mg/dL pre-dawn rise.
Overnight Stability
Glucose should remain relatively flat during deep sleep (10 PM – 3 AM). Elevated overnight variability often correlates with late-night eating or poor sleep quality. Target: SD <10 mg/dL overnight.
Sleep Architecture × Glucose
Deep sleep stages correlate with the most stable glucose. REM sleep shows slight increases likely tied to cortisol cycling. Nights with >90 min deep sleep show lower morning fasting glucose the next day.

Genomic Context
Why my glucose curve is mine
Genetic variants in FADS2 (fatty acid desaturase) and MTHFR (methylation pathway) affect how individuals metabolize carbohydrates and process glucose differently. Population averages for glycemic index don’t account for these variants — which is exactly why N=1 CGM data matters more than any food label.
The Anxiety Resolution

Health anxiety doesn’t respond to reassurance. It responds to evidence. Before the CGM, I carried a low-grade dread that my weight meant my blood sugar was silently destroying my health. No doctor visit could fully quiet that voice, because a single fasting number leaves too much room for catastrophic thinking.

Wearing a continuous monitor changed the equation. Instead of imagining the worst, I could see exactly what was happening — 288 times a day. The data didn’t just show that my glucose was more stable than I feared. It gave me a framework to understand what moves the number and what doesn’t.

The CGM didn’t cure the anxiety. But it replaced speculation with observation, and that turned out to be enough.

288 readings per day
vs one fasting number

A fasting glucose test gives you one snapshot taken after 8+ hours without food. It captures none of the metabolic action: not what happens after meals, not whether glucose spikes at 2am, not whether a salad or a steak drives different post-meal curves in your body specifically.

A continuous glucose monitor reads glucose every 5 minutes, 24 hours a day. The additional signal includes glucose variability, post-meal spike magnitude, nocturnal patterns, and how specific foods interact with your individual physiology — not population averages.

Time-in-range is now considered a more actionable metric than HbA1c because it responds to intervention within days, not months.

Measurement Protocol
SENSOR Dexcom Stelo OTC CGM. 15-day wear cycle. 5-minute reading interval. No calibration required. 288 data points per day.
TIME IN RANGE (TIR) Standard: 70–180 mg/dL (ADA guideline). Optimal: 70–120 mg/dL (longevity medicine target). Target: >90% standard, >70% optimal.
VARIABILITY (SD) Standard deviation of all 5-min readings in a 24-hour period. SD <15: excellent. 15–20: good. >20: elevated metabolic stress. N=1 data — individual baselines vary.

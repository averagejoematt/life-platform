How experiments are designed
1
Hypothesis
State a falsifiable prediction: "If I add 10g of creatine daily for 4 weeks, I expect fasting glucose to remain stable while lean mass increases by at least 0.5 lbs." The specific prediction is set before starting.
2
Protocol
Define the exact intervention, start date, and duration. Establish a baseline window (7–14 days of data before the intervention begins). No mid-experiment changes to variables.
3
Measurement
Identify the primary outcome metric and which data source tracks it. Secondary metrics are pre-specified, not cherry-picked after results come in.
4
Result
Compare the experiment window against the pre-experiment baseline. Publish regardless of outcome. Negative results are as informative as positive ones.
What makes a good N=1 experiment
Control for the obvious confounders. Don't start a sleep experiment during a travel week. Don't test a training protocol while also changing diet. One variable at a time — or explicitly note when you can't.
Minimum duration matched to the outcome. HRV changes can be visible in 1–2 weeks. Body composition changes need 4+ weeks to clear noise. Lab biomarkers need 8–12 weeks. Match the window to the biology.
Pre-register the hypothesis. Deciding what "success" looks like after seeing the data is how self-experiments become confirmation bias. The hypothesis is locked before Day 1.
Use an objective metric. "I feel better" isn't a result. "HRV increased from 58ms baseline to 67ms average over the experiment window" is a result. The platform's 19 live data sources make objective outcomes the default.
N=1 is still useful. Between-person variance is the enemy of population studies. Single-subject experiments bypass it entirely — the person is their own control. The limitation is generalisability, not signal quality.

On the methodology

These are N=1 experiments — single-subject self-experiments with no control group. The scientific limitations are real: no blinding, no randomization, seasonal confounders, regression to the mean. Results apply to me specifically and may not generalize.

What makes them useful anyway: consistency of measurement. With 19 live data sources running continuously, the baseline data is dense enough that meaningful signal can emerge from even a single-person study — particularly for outcomes with low day-to-day variance like resting HRV and fasting glucose.

Experiments are tracked inside the Life Platform. Each one has a defined start date, hypothesis variables, and auto-comparison against the pre-experiment window. Results are published here unchanged — including the ones that didn't work.

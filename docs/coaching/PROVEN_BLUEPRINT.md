## The cut, in one line

**5 Sep 2024 (307.2 lb) → 30 Apr 2025 (188.8 lb): −118 lb in 34 weeks (~3.5 lb/wk),
then a full regain to ~307 by Jun 2026.** The loss was remarkably *steady* — 12–19 lb every
single month, no stall. The regain came after, and (per the failure-mode register) was
psychological, not metabolic.

The headline: **the cut was driven by enormous *easy* volume, not intensity.** Average walk
HR across the whole period was **97 bpm** — genuinely conversational. Volume did the work.

## Cross-episode finding (2012–2026) — the holding problem is the whole game

Mining the full 14-year weight history (turning-point detection on smoothed weight, joined to
Strava/Hevy) surfaces **16 distinct loss episodes ≥15 lb — and 0 that held** (held = regained <⅓ of
the loss within 6 months). Losses include 36, 40, 69, 74 and 116 lb. **Losing has never been the
problem; holding has never once been solved.**

- **Regain is nearly as fast as loss.** Loss ≈ 3.0 lb/wk, regain ≈ 2.4 lb/wk (0.79×). The slow
  post-cut drift that *should* follow doesn't — weight returns almost as fast as it left.
- **Mechanism = post-trough activity collapse.** Walking volume craters in the ~8 weeks after every
  low (2024–25: 11.4 → 4.4 walks/wk, still decaying). The loss engine is switched off at the moment
  of success.
- **It's seasonal.** 7 of 16 cuts start in December — a New-Year-restart loop that dies by mid-year.

*Rigor:* n is small and training co-variates are only reliable from ~2020 (sparse Strava before —
zeros there mean "not logged," not "inactive"). Descriptive of his own history, correlational.

**Implication for the program:** the maintenance/defense phase and the post-trough activity-floor
early-warning (§"Where the science improves", items 1 & 3) are not nice-to-haves — they are the *only*
thing standing between a 17th successful loss and a 15th regain. Frame forward (compassionate,
never a failure tally): "loss is the part you've proven; holding is the part we're engineering this
time."

## What the engine actually was

During the 34-week cut, per week, on average:
- **~22 logged sessions/week, ~21 hours of activity/week.**
- **Walking: ~11–12 walks/week, ~1 hr each** — the backbone (386 walks, ~370 hrs total).
- **Lifting: 4.5 sessions/week, ~128 working sets/week** — squat/bench/deadlift the top three
  movements by volume, ~10-rep work.
- **Running: introduced only once under ~240 lb**, then scaled to 5–6/week.
- **Structure: frequently two-a-day** — AM lift (☀️) + a PM session (💪), the PM piece usually the walk.

This matches the calibration's capacity notes (sustained 2-hr-gym + daily-cardio blocks) — and
now it's quantified.

set the floor for what's known-tolerable at Matthew's current weight, then let readiness/joints

2. **Running is joint-gated at ~240 lb.** *Zero* runs logged above 240; it appears at 240 and
   scales down-weight. This is the empirical basis for the calibration's bodyweight-tier run gate.

## The lift blueprint (what the iron looked like)

- **Frequency:** high — each big lift hit multiple times/week inside a full-body / mixed daily structure
  ("Morning workout ☀️" + "Afternoon workout 💪"), not a strict split.
- **Centerpiece movements (by set volume):** Squat (Barbell), Bench Press (Barbell), Deadlift (Barbell),
  Hip Thrust, Overhead Press, Bent-Over Row, Shrug, Reverse Lunge, plus unilateral leg work
  (Bulgarian split squat, sprinter lunges) and standard arm/shoulder accessories.
- **Rep range:** median 10 reps on working sets — hypertrophy-led, not powerlifting-low.
- **Loads:** ramped fast from the re-entry weeks, then **held roughly flat across the entire cut** —
  ≈225 squat, ≈225 bench, 250–289 deadlift, ~135–145 OHP, ~185–225 row from 260 lb down to 190 lb.
  Holding load through a 118-lb loss is *decent* muscle defense.

## Where the science improves on what he did

The data is clear that Matthew's failure mode is **holding, not losing**. He has proven, twice, that
he can drive a steep, sustained loss. The regain is where it breaks. So the upgrades are not
"train harder" — he maxed that — they are:

1. **Run a more defensible rate, with planned diet breaks and a maintenance/defense phase.**
   3.5 lb/wk is above the platform's own muscle-loss rate flag (>2.5 lb/wk), and the cut had no
   planned landing — it ran flat-out until it reversed. Target a rate that protects lean mass and
   *schedule* refeeds/diet-breaks plus an explicit maintenance block at the end. The platform now has
   the instruments the last cut lacked: `get_deficit_sustainability`, `get_metabolic_adaptation`,
   journal sentiment/mood trajectory. (Nutrition stays qualitative and anti-restriction per the
   wellness guardrail — this is "eat enough to hold the loss," never "eat less.")
2. **Periodize the loads he held flat.** Keep the high-frequency big-3 — it works for him — but wave
   the intensity (heavy / medium / light blocks) instead of holding ~225 static. That's the upgrade
   from "muscle maintained" to "muscle gained while cutting."
3. **Protect the easy PM session as easy, and treat mood/continuity as the regain firewall.** The
   two-a-day worked *because* the second session was a walk, not a second grind — encode AM-hard /
   PM-easy. And the #1 documented risk (mood-driven continuity) is exactly what the last cut had no
   monitoring for; the journal/mood tools are the early-warning system this time.

## How the coach uses this doc

- **Set the volume floor from the band table**, then autoregulate *down* from it. At 307 lb today, the
  blueprint says ~10 easy walks/week + re-entry lifting + **no running** — and Matthew is currently at
  roughly *one* walk/week. Closing that walking gap is the first, highest-leverage move, ahead of any
  lift programming.
- **Enforce the run gate** at ~240 lb from his own history, not as a generic caution.
- **Frame progress against his own proven trajectory** ("last time at this weight you were doing X"),
  which is both more motivating and more honest than textbook targets.
- **Hold the three improvements as standing redlines** so this cut is engineered to be *held*, not just
  achieved.

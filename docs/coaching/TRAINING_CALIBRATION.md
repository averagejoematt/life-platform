## 0. Bias correction (read first)

The default coach stance has historically been **too conservative** for this athlete.
Work capacity AND fatigue tolerance are exceptional — he has sustained ~8-month blocks of
2 hr gym + daily afternoon aerobic work (ruck/run/walk), **twice**, and has never quit from
physical fatigue. The binding constraints are NOT volume, intensity, or recovery from hard
work. They are:

1. **Injury / connective-tissue re-adaptation** on novel-again movements (tendons + joints
   lag muscle and CV adaptation by weeks; age + weight-cycling raise the stakes). This is
   the *only* physical thing that has the potential to end a block — protect it.
2. **Mood-driven continuity** — the variable that has actually made or broken every gain.
   Psychological, not physical.

Calibrate against *those two things*, not against an imagined fragility. When in doubt
between "push" and "hold," the tie goes to **push on frequency/volume**, and **hold only
on tendon-limited novel patterns, time-boxed.**

## 2. Failure-mode register  *(the actual enemies of fast results)*

**Correction (2026-06, athlete-stated):** the cliff is *psychological, not physical*.
Athlete has sustained extreme training loads (2 hr gym + afternoon ruck/run/walk, daily,
with rehab-day training) for ~8-month blocks **twice**, and has **never** reverted to
sedentary from overtraining or physical fatigue. Both reversals were triggered by
**bereavement / depression / acute stress**, not training. When "on," he is reliably on and
pushes through.

- **Primary risk — mood-driven discontinuity:** the single highest-leverage variable. The
  threat is the off-switch, which is psychological and may deserve its own support strategy
  *outside* training design.
- **Secondary risk — injury, not fatigue:** the one *physical* event that could end a block —
  and the one he hasn't hit. With age + repeated weight-cycling, connective tissue / joints
  are the asset to protect. This is the real reason for the narrow tendon guardrail, not any
  doubt about work capacity.
- **NOT a failure mode for this athlete:** physical overreaching, "too much volume," a
  week-3 fatigue cliff. **Do not program against these.** He tolerates volume that would
  break most people.
- **Physical quirks to program around:** (a) **Ab/core spasms** — direct/compressive ab work
  (e.g. planks, hard crunching) triggers cramps, especially early. Use standing / anti-movement
  core (Pallof, carries, chops) and let braced compounds cover the rest. (b) **Current mobility
  deficit** at present bodyweight — early phase needs accessible, low-friction options
  (recumbent bike over saddle bikes; mat mobility; no high-impact yet).

## 3. Calibration philosophy

**Aggressive where safe; conservative only where tissue-limited, and only time-boxed.**

- **Frequency:** progress aggressively. High-frequency, moderate-duration beats
  low-frequency heroics for this athlete.
- **Volume:** ramp weekly sets-per-muscle from a *real* baseline — do not park at fixed
  low sets. Add sets/load week over week while recovery markers hold.
- **Load:** **performance-gated**, not blanket subtract-only. Prescribe a feeler floor;
  climb *within* the session if it moves at target RIR. Once a lift has a current baseline,
  prescribe near it.
- **Novel-again patterns** (e.g., legs after a long layoff): **1–2 week submaximal on-ramp**
  to let tendons + pattern catch up, then open up. The on-ramp is *earned out of fast*,
  not a permanent ceiling.

## 3a. The coaching edge — why programmed 200 days should beat self-directed 200 days

The job is **optimization over an already-able athlete**, not restraint. His prior
self-directed high-effort blocks worked, but left gains on the table that are NOT "do more":

- **Effective volume, not junk volume** — fewer, harder, well-placed sets; same or better
  adaptation, less wasted fatigue. (2 hr daily ≠ 2 hr of productive stimulus.)
- **Polarized cardio** — most aerobic work truly easy (Z2), a small dose hard; avoid the
  gray-zone middle his prior daily ruck/run/walk likely lived in. Better aerobic return/hour.
- **Structured progression + planned deloads** — beats "hard every day," which plateaus.
- **Autoregulation** — push *harder* than self-selection on green days, ease only when data
  says compromised. Raises the ceiling; does not lower it.

Edge = precision + allocation of his (large) recovery budget to highest-yield stimuli, plus
injury avoidance to protect continuity. **Caveat:** biggest determinant of outcome is still
whether he stays "on" (see §2) — training design supports that but does not solve it.

## 4. Autoregulation gates — push hard *inside* these

**GO harder when ALL of:**
- Readiness GREEN
- HRV at or above baseline
- Sleep at/above target (duration + efficiency)
- No joint/tendon pain (muscular burn is fine)
- Prescribed loads moving at ≥ target RIR

**HOLD / cut to floor when ANY of:**
- Sharp or localized **joint/tendon** pain (not muscular) → stop that movement
- Readiness RED two consecutive days → floor variant that day
- HRV sustained drop (>~10–15% off baseline) across several days
- Sleep duration/efficiency degrading multi-day
- RIR collapsing — can't hit prescribed reps at prescribed load → systemic fatigue flag

**NEVER (regardless of motivation):**
- Max-out / train to failure on **session 1 of a novel-again pattern**
- Two heavy **axial** patterns (e.g., heavy squat + heavy conventional pull) cold in one session
- Skip the readiness check because you "feel fine" — today's score is **pre-strain** and
  doesn't reflect today's work

## 5c. Bodyweight-tier progression model (athlete's own mental model — formalize it)

He naturally frames blocks by bodyweight: as he gets lighter/fitter, he unlocks harder,
more diverse, higher-skill/impact work. Use this as the gating logic — it aligns his
ambition with the injury mandate:

- **Heaviest (now):** low-impact, joint-sparing — bike (recumbent), machines, supported
  free weights, landmine, sled/carries, mobility. **No plyo, no running yet** (impact load
  on joints at high bodyweight is the injury vector). Build the base + the streak.
- **Mid:** introduce jump rope, light plyo, brisk incline walks/rucks, KB complexes, more
  free-weight skill work, run-walk intervals.
- **Lighter:** full plyometrics, running volume, boxing/agility, higher-skill Oly work,
  performance benchmarks back on the table.
- Gate transitions on **bodyweight + joint signal + the autoregulation gates (§4)**, not on
  calendar alone.

## 5d. Hevy build standard (how every routine ships)

**Every exercise carries a purposeful note/comment.** No bare sets. Notes should teach
intent, not just prescribe. Specifically:

- **Cardio is prescribed with an explicit *intent* per session, and varied across the week.**
  Spell out the workout. Examples of the *range* to rotate through:
  - *Recovery spin:* steady low level, same resistance ~40 min, loosen legs / flush.
  - *Hill profile:* simulate a climb up-and-over, varying resistance.
  - *Intervals:* get used to HR climbing — work/rest structure stated.
  - *Long hard effort:* a deliberate slog at a high steady level.
- **Accessory lifts:** give a focus cue — what to feel, tempo, a pause/hold at a position,
  ROM emphasis, or a target muscle to "own."
- **Rest as a programmed variable:** state rest, and **manipulate it over weeks** (e.g.,
  trim rest or add reps vs prior week to progress without always adding load). Call out when
  a rest period is a good moment for a mobility drill / stretch.
- **Warm-up cues** before key lifts (e.g., ramp + activation before squats).
- **If heavy lifting isn't the call** that day (readiness/joint/phase): don't default to
  "less" — fill the 2-hr block productively with **accessory work or structured
  cardio-machine sessions** that still serve the mission.

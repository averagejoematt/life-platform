### Dr. Rhonda Patrick 🧬 — Micronutrients, Genome & Longevity

The omega-3 index is what I want to talk about. It went from 7.8% last year to 3.3% this year. That is a catastrophic failure of repletion, and I want to be clear about what it means and why it happened.

Below 4% omega-3 index is associated with the same all-cause mortality risk as smoking. Your number is 3.3%. Last year you were at 7.8%, which is in the cardioprotective range. Something between those two draws caused you to lose more than half of your omega-3 incorporation into red blood cell membranes. In twelve months. That doesn't happen from "not enough fish." That happens from one of three things: you stopped supplementing, the supplement went rancid (omega-3 oxidation is real and a 2-year-old fish oil bottle is probably worse than nothing), or you switched to ALA-based plant supplementation, which **with your FADS2 A;G genotype converts at 26.7% lower efficiency.**

I'd bet on the second or third. Whatever the cause, the prescription is the same and it is non-negotiable: **3 grams per day of EPA+DHA from a third-party-tested liquid fish oil**, not capsules, not gummies, not algae unless you're vegetarian. The omega-3 liquid you ordered — verify the EPA+DHA total per serving. Many "1000mg fish oil" capsules contain 300mg of actual EPA+DHA. Read the back label.

Vitamin D is the second one. You went from 117 ng/mL (high — too high, frankly) to 28 ng/mL (deficient). With your CYP2R1 G;G, your set point is genuinely lower than average and you require a higher maintenance dose to land in the optimal 40-60 ng/mL range. The collapse from 117 to 28 means you stopped supplementing entirely, plus you spent the winter in Seattle. Resume 5000 IU daily with K2 (your VKORC1 sensitivity makes the K2 matter). Recheck in 8 weeks. Target is 50, not 100.

Choline I want to flag because no one else will. You have MTHFD1 + MTRR + PEMT — the triple risk. Your dietary choline target is 550mg+. Almost no one hits that without eggs or organ meat. Three whole eggs daily is the simplest answer.

On methylation: your homocysteine should be checked next draw if it's not on the standing panel. With MTHFR, you should be on methylated folate (Thorne Methyl-Guard Plus is a fine choice — I see Matthew already selected this). Take it.

The thread connecting all of this: your micronutrient status is the floor that everything else is built on. You can't out-train, out-statin, or out-deficit a micronutrient deficiency. **Repletion first, optimization second.**

### Dr. Andrew Huberman 🔬 — Neuroscience & Protocols

I want to come at this from a different angle than my colleagues.

Insulin sensitivity is not just a metabolic story. It is a circadian story. The single most powerful behavioral lever for insulin sensitivity that nobody talks about is **morning sunlight within 30 minutes of waking.** This sets the cortisol awakening response, which sets the diurnal cortisol curve, which regulates evening insulin sensitivity. Seattle in winter makes this harder, but you have a 10,000 lux SAD lamp option that I'd be aggressive about during the dark months.

Second lever: **Zone 2 cardiac training.** You're already on this. But specifically for insulin sensitivity, what matters is the post-meal walk. A 10-minute walk within 30 minutes of finishing a meal reduces postprandial glucose excursion by ~30% in metabolically compromised individuals. It is the highest-ROI behavioral intervention for insulin resistance that requires zero new equipment, zero new supplement, and three minutes of habit formation. Stack it onto your existing CGM feedback loop.

Third: **sleep architecture.** Your Whoop and Eight Sleep data show your sleep quality. Insulin sensitivity drops 20-30% after a single night of restricted sleep. With Eight Sleep, you have temperature control. Get serious about the cool-down protocol — 65°F by 10pm, body temperature is the master regulator of sleep onset for someone who runs warm.

I'm not going to argue with Peter on the statin. I will say: the protocol stack of (morning sunlight + post-meal walks + Zone 2 4-5x/week + temperature-regulated sleep) is what the IR score should be re-measured against. If after 90 days of strict protocol stack adherence the IR score doesn't move, then I'm fully on board with metformin and any other escalation. But I think this stack alone moves the IR score from 75 to ~50 in 90 days. We should give the protocol a chance to do its work before adding pharmacology to the equation.

The risk I see in this room is the temptation to pharmacologize everything. The protocol stack is not weaker than metformin for early IR. It's just less easily packaged.
